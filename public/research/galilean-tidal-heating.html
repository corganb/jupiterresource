Galilean moon tidal heating budgets: from Peale 1979 to Galileo and Juno

Review of the tidal-heating energy budgets for Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto, from the prediction of Io's volcanism to the present-day constraints from Galileo and Juno mass-loss and gravity-field measurements.
2026-04-25 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

The Galilean moons Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto are locked in the Laplace 4:2:1 mean-motion resonance, which forces orbital eccentricities and produces sustained tidal heating against Jupiter's gravitational potential. Tidal heating has shaped the moons' present-day geology and habitability potential. This report reviews the heating budgets and the observational constraints from Voyager, Galileo, and Juno.

Peale prediction and the Voyager confirmation

Peale, Cassen, and Reynolds (1979) predicted that Io should be the most volcanically active body in the Solar System on the basis of computed tidal heating in the Io-Europa-Ganymede resonance. The prediction was published in March 1979, weeks before the Voyager 1 flyby of Io in March 1979 imaged the first active extraterrestrial volcanism (Loki, Pele, and others). The Peale et al. paper remains a landmark in predictive planetary science.

Io: the dominant heating budget

Io's present-day total heat output from tidal dissipation is estimated at approximately 10^14 watts. This is approximately 30 times Earth's total internal heat flow on a per-area basis and is sufficient to maintain Io's silicate mantle in a partially molten state. Voyager and Galileo imaging has documented over 400 active volcanoes and surface temperatures up to 1900 K at active vents. Io's volcanic resurfacing rate is estimated at 1 cm per year averaged globally, two orders of magnitude faster than any other Solar-System body.

The dissipation occurs primarily in Io's silicate interior, with the mantle's effective viscosity setting the steady-state heat flow. Detailed thermal models constrained by surface-temperature distributions favour a thin asthenosphere or magma ocean at depth. Direct constraints from Galileo gravity measurements limit the global ocean thickness if present.

Europa: tidal heating and the subsurface ocean

Europa's tidal heating is approximately 10^11 to 10^12 watts - two to three orders of magnitude smaller than Io's. The dissipation occurs in Europa's silicate mantle, in its subsurface ocean (where mechanical work against viscous flow generates heat), and in the overlying ice shell during deformation. Galileo magnetometer data confirmed the presence of a global subsurface ocean from induced-magnetic-field measurements (Khurana et al. 1998). The ocean depth is estimated at 100 km below an ice shell of 20-30 km thickness.

Europa's tidal heat budget is sufficient to maintain the subsurface ocean against freezing on geologic timescales, but the heat is unevenly distributed: the ice shell at the equator is thinner than at the poles, and chaos terrain is interpreted as locations where the shell has thinned to near-melting locally. The Europa Clipper mission (arrival 2030) will refine these estimates with magnetometer, ice-penetrating radar, and gravity measurements.

Ganymede and Callisto: declining heating

Ganymede's tidal heating is approximately 10^10 to 10^11 watts, with significant uncertainty because Ganymede sits at the outer edge of the Laplace resonance and the resonant forcing is reduced. Galileo data confirmed Ganymede has a subsurface salty ocean below an ice shell, and the moon possesses an intrinsic magnetic field generated in a metallic core. The ESA JUICE mission (arrival 2031) will provide the first orbital reconnaissance of Ganymede and refine the interior structure.

Callisto receives negligible tidal heating from the Laplace resonance and is dominated by primordial-heat retention. Galileo data indicate Callisto also has a subsurface ocean (induced-magnetic-field measurement) but at greater depth than Europa or Ganymede. Callisto's surface is the oldest and most heavily cratered among the Galileans, consistent with the absence of recent geological activity.

Resonance stability and orbital evolution

The Laplace 4:2:1 resonance is stable on timescales much longer than the Solar System age, but its detailed orbital parameters evolve with tidal dissipation. Lainey et al. (2009) used Galileo astrometry to measure tidal acceleration of Io's orbit relative to Jupiter, finding that Io is migrating outward at approximately 4 cm per year while Jupiter's tidal-Q is much smaller than predicted from purely viscoelastic models. The implication is that Jupiter's Q has a strong frequency dependence and that the planet's interior dynamics permit efficient tidal dissipation at Io's forcing frequency.

Outlook

Juno's extended mission has executed close approaches to Ganymede (June 2021) and Europa (September 2022) and is planned for two close approaches to Io in 2023-2024 at minimum altitude approximately 1500 km. The Juno data add gravity-coefficient measurements that constrain interior structure beyond Galileo-era values. The combination of Juno, Europa Clipper, and JUICE will provide an unprecedented multi-mission interior-structure data set across the Galileans by the early 2030s.

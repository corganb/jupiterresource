JUICE and Europa Clipper: target observation plans for Jovian icy moons

Review of the science objectives and target-observation plans for ESA's JUICE (Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer) and NASA's Europa Clipper missions, the Ganymede vs Europa-vs-Callisto observational priorities, and the cross-mission coordination for shared Jovian-system science.
2026-04-28 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

The Jovian icy moons - Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto - are leading targets for outer-Solar-System astrobiology and oceanography because all three have evidence of subsurface liquid-water oceans. The 2022 launch of ESA's Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (JUICE) and the 2024 launch of NASA's Europa Clipper constitute the largest investment in outer-Solar-System exploration since the Voyager missions. The two missions have complementary primary targets and overlapping flyby coverage of the broader Jovian system. This report reviews the mission architectures, the target-observation plans, and the cross-mission coordination.

JUICE

JUICE launched in April 2023 (delayed from the 2022 baseline) and arrives at Jupiter in July 2031. The mission profile includes 35 flybys of the Galilean moons (12 of Ganymede, 21 of Callisto, 2 of Europa) followed by Ganymede orbit insertion in late 2034. JUICE is the first dedicated outer-planet moon orbiter (after the Cassini mission, which orbited Saturn rather than a moon).

The JUICE primary target is Ganymede, the largest moon in the Solar System and the only moon known to have an intrinsic magnetic field. The science objectives include: detailed characterisation of the suspected subsurface liquid-water ocean; investigation of the surface and interior structure; understanding the magnetic field generation; and Jovian-system-context science from the Callisto and Europa flybys. The instrument suite includes 10 science instruments covering imaging, spectroscopy, radar, particle and plasma instrumentation, and radio-science.

Europa Clipper

Europa Clipper launched in October 2024 and arrives at Jupiter in April 2030. The mission profile is a series of approximately 50 close flybys of Europa from a Jupiter-orbiting trajectory that minimises radiation-environment exposure (avoiding sustained orbit around Europa, which would expose the spacecraft to lethal radiation doses). Each flyby surveys a different region of Europa's surface.

The primary target is Europa's habitability potential: characterising the liquid-water ocean beneath the ice shell, the ice-shell thickness and composition, and the surface processes that could sample ocean material. The instrument suite includes 9 science instruments, with the Europa Imaging System for high-resolution surface imaging, the Europa Thermal Emission Imaging System for surface composition mapping, the REASON radar for ice-shell structure, the SUDA dust mass spectrometer for surface-particle composition, and the Mass Spectrometer for Planetary Exploration for any plume-emitted material.

Cross-mission coordination

The two missions overlap in time at Jupiter from 2030 (Europa Clipper arrival) through approximately 2035 (end of JUICE's Ganymede orbital phase). The coordination is formalised through a NASA-ESA Joint Science Working Group that has identified shared observational programmes. Key cross-mission opportunities include: simultaneous Europa observations from JUICE flybys and Europa Clipper flybys (allowing two-spacecraft coverage of magnetic-field disturbance from the moon); shared calibration of the radar instruments through repeat-coverage of the same surface targets; and joint atmospheric monitoring of Jupiter during simultaneous flybys of the planet.

Europa-specific science

Europa Clipper's primary objective is determining whether Europa's subsurface ocean is habitable. The principal observational thread is constraining the chemistry, composition, and thickness of the ice shell, plus any surface evidence of recent ocean-surface communication (water plumes, geologically-recent surface features). The mission does not directly probe the ocean (which would require a melt-through lander), but it provides the global characterisation that would precede any future lander mission. Europa Lander concept studies have been conducted by NASA for over a decade; no funded follow-on mission is in development.

Ganymede-specific science

JUICE's Ganymede orbital phase will provide the first detailed magnetic-field mapping of an icy moon, surface composition mapping at meter-scale resolution, gravity-field measurement, and ice-shell radar sounding. The mission will determine whether Ganymede's intrinsic magnetic field can produce auroral signatures of subsurface ocean salinity and depth. Ganymede orbital phase concludes with controlled deorbit into the surface, providing high-resolution late-mission imaging.

Callisto-specific science

Callisto's role in both missions is more limited: 21 flybys for JUICE and one or more flybys (depending on final trajectory design) for Europa Clipper. Callisto has the lowest a priori habitability potential of the three icy Galilean moons (the suspected ocean is deep beneath a thick ice shell), but it provides the cleanest geological-context measurement: Callisto's surface is the most heavily-cratered and geologically-stable of the Galilean moons, providing a benchmark against which the more dynamic Europa and Ganymede surfaces can be calibrated.

Outlook

The combined JUICE and Europa Clipper data sets will dominate outer-Solar-System science from 2030 through approximately 2040. Beyond these missions, no major outer-Solar-System mission is currently in advanced development; the Uranus orbiter and the Neptune-Triton orbiter both ranked highly in the 2023 Decadal Survey but require multi-decade lead times for development. The icy-moons exploration thread will be advanced primarily through these two missions during the 2030s.

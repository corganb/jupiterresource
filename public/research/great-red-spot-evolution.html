Great Red Spot size evolution and convective dynamics

Review of the Great Red Spot's measured size evolution from 1879 to 2026, the recent acceleration of shrinkage observed since 2012, the Juno deep-structure constraints, and the convective and barotropic-instability mechanisms proposed for the storm's persistence and current decline.
2026-04-28 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

The Great Red Spot is the longest-observed atmospheric vortex in the Solar System, with continuous observation since 1879. The storm has decreased in size by approximately 50 percent over the observed record, with notable acceleration of the shrinkage in the past decade. The cause of the persistence and the current decline are active research questions; recent Juno deep-atmospheric measurements have constrained the storm's vertical structure and dynamics. This report reviews the size-evolution record and the dynamical interpretation.

Historical size record

Visual observations from Earth-based telescopes establish the size record. In 1879, the Great Red Spot extended approximately 41,000 km in zonal extent (longitudinal direction) at approximately 22 degrees south latitude. By 1980, the zonal extent had shrunk to approximately 25,000 km. By 2014, when Hubble Space Telescope imagery began the modern high-precision record, the zonal extent was approximately 16,500 km, and the meridional extent (north-south) was approximately 11,000 km. By 2024, zonal extent had declined to approximately 12,500 km, with the meridional extent largely unchanged - the storm has become more circular as it has shrunk.

Modern monitoring

The Hubble Space Telescope's Outer Planet Atmospheres Legacy (OPAL) programme has produced annual high-resolution imagery of the Great Red Spot since 2014, providing the most precise modern size record. Juno's JunoCam imaging has produced higher-resolution but less-frequent imagery during its perijove passes; the JunoCam dataset captures small-scale features and storm-edge behaviour that Hubble does not resolve. Combined with ground-based amateur and professional imaging, the modern record provides approximately weekly cadence on the storm's size and shape.

Vertical structure from Juno

The Juno MWR observations of the Great Red Spot reveal that the storm extends to approximately 300 km depth in the troposphere - much deeper than the visible cloud deck and substantially deeper than equivalent storms in Earth's atmosphere. The deep-structure inference is independently supported by Juno's gravity-field measurements, which detect the gravity signature of the Great Red Spot's mass anomaly extending into the molecular-hydrogen envelope. The depth of the storm provides part of the explanation for its longevity: a deep-rooted vortex is more stable against shear-induced disruption than a shallow one.

Dynamical persistence

The persistence of the Great Red Spot is at the limit of what current dynamical models can reproduce. The prevailing interpretation is a balance between vortex-merging (smaller storms in the same latitude band episodically merge into the Great Red Spot, supplying angular momentum) and shear-and-shedding loss (the surrounding zonal flow strips material from the storm edges). The balance has been approximately maintained for at least the 145 years of continuous observation, but the recent acceleration of shrinkage suggests the loss term has become dominant.

Recent shrinkage acceleration

The 2012-2024 shrinkage rate has been approximately 4 percent per year, substantially faster than the long-term average. The acceleration coincides with several observed changes in the storm's behaviour: increased rotation rate (now approximately 60 m/s), sharpening of the storm-edge wind shear, and small-scale red-flake-shedding events at the storm edges. The shedding events, captured in Hubble and JunoCam imagery, transfer mass and vorticity from the Great Red Spot to the surrounding atmosphere and are interpreted as the principal current loss mechanism.

Future evolution

Linear extrapolation of the recent shrinkage rate would predict the storm shrinking to a Earth-diameter scale (approximately 12,000 km) by 2026 and to dissipation by approximately 2050. Linear extrapolation is unreliable in non-linear dynamical systems; the storm may stabilise at a smaller size, undergo further acceleration of decline, or transition to a different morphology. The observed circularisation of the storm shape during shrinkage may reflect a transition to a more stable configuration, or may be a precursor to fission. The evolution will be tracked through continued Hubble OPAL observations and through JWST imagery campaigns, with substantially longer time baselines than Juno's mission lifetime.

Outlook

Continued Hubble OPAL coverage and JWST infrared observations will track the storm's evolution through the coming decade. The Europa Clipper mission, while targeted at the Jovian moons, includes Jupiter-system-context imaging during Jupiter flybys that will add data points. The proposed Jupiter atmosphere descent probes (e.g. the JIVE concept) would provide direct in-situ measurement of the storm's thermodynamic structure if any such mission is funded. The dynamical theory of giant-planet vortex persistence remains an active field with implications for atmospheric dynamics on Saturn (the Polar Hexagon), Neptune (the Great Dark Spot, since dispersed), and on exoplanets where direct imaging is approaching the resolution to detect analogous storms.

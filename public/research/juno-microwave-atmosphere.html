Juno deep atmospheric microwave probing of Jupiter

Review of the Juno Microwave Radiometer (MWR) results on Jupiter's deep atmosphere, the implications for ammonia abundance and meridional structure, the deep-water-cloud absent-detection result, and the constraints on dynamics below the visible cloud deck.
2026-04-28 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

NASA's Juno mission has been in Jupiter polar orbit since July 2016. The Microwave Radiometer (MWR) instrument, the principal direct probe of Jupiter's deep atmosphere, measures atmospheric thermal emission at six microwave frequencies (1.4, 2.6, 5.2, 10, 22, and 600 GHz) corresponding to weighting functions that peak at successively-deeper pressure levels. The MWR data set is the first direct in-situ probe of Jupiter's atmosphere below the ammonia-cloud deck and has substantially revised the picture of the deep-atmosphere structure. This report reviews the principal MWR results and their implications for Jupiter dynamics.

Instrument design and weighting functions

The six MWR channels probe pressure levels from approximately 1 bar (top of the cloud deck) down to approximately 100 bar (deep beneath the visible cloud structure). The weighting functions overlap, allowing differential analysis to extract abundance and temperature profiles as functions of depth. The instrument design is optimised for the ammonia-rich atmosphere; ammonia is the principal microwave absorber in the relevant frequency range, and the channel selection allows simultaneous retrieval of ammonia abundance and atmospheric temperature.

Ammonia abundance and meridional structure

Pre-Juno expectations were for a relatively well-mixed deep atmosphere with ammonia abundance approaching the deep-source-region value below the cloud-condensation level. The MWR data showed instead a strongly meridionally-structured ammonia distribution: ammonia is depleted in the equatorial zones but enhanced in the equatorial deep belts and substantially-variable across other latitude bands. The depletion extends well below the ammonia condensation level, indicating dynamic transport effects rather than condensation-induced depletion alone.

The interpretation favoured by the Juno team is that "mushballs" - hailstone-like aggregates of ammonia and water ice formed in lightning-driven convection - sediment into the deep atmosphere and are mixed by deep meridional circulation. The mushball mechanism explains the depletion magnitude, the meridional structure, and the connection to the observed lightning distribution in the equatorial zones. The hypothesis is now broadly accepted but the quantitative model parameters are still being refined.

The deep water question

The deep water abundance is a long-standing constraint on Jupiter formation: a high water abundance favours formation in colder regions of the protoplanetary nebula or with specific accretion mechanisms. Juno's MWR was expected to provide the first direct measurement of deep water abundance through the 1.4 and 2.6 GHz channels. The actual result is a complicated picture: the deep water abundance varies regionally, with the equatorial zones showing higher water abundance than the mid-latitudes. The current best estimate of the global deep water abundance is approximately 2-4 times the solar value, consistent with formation expectations but with substantial uncertainty.

Deep dynamics

The MWR ammonia and water structure imply meridional circulation patterns that extend much deeper than predicted by pre-Juno models. The depth of the zonal-jet structure has been independently constrained by the Juno gravity-field measurement to extend to approximately 3,000 km depth, consistent with the deep-circulation picture. The combined gravity-field and MWR data set has produced a revised model of Jupiter's interior dynamics in which the visible cloud-level zonal jets are surface manifestations of much deeper circulation cells extending into the molecular hydrogen envelope.

Storm-cell dynamics

Targeted MWR observations during Juno close-approach passes over individual storm cells (the Great Red Spot, smaller anticyclonic storms, the polar cyclone clusters) have characterised the deep structure of these features. The Great Red Spot, in particular, has thermal-and-ammonia structure extending to approximately 300 km depth, much deeper than implied by the visible-cloud structure alone. The polar cyclone clusters - persistent multi-cyclone structures unique to Jupiter among the gas giants - have shallower depth structure and are interpreted as upper-troposphere phenomena rather than connections to the deep circulation.

Implications for Jovian formation

The combined MWR and gravity-field results constrain Jupiter's interior structure, formation history, and evolution. The deep water abundance has implications for the snow-line position during Jupiter's formation and the mechanism of gas-giant gas accretion. The deep zonal-jet structure constrains the heat-transport-driven convection in the molecular hydrogen envelope. The persistence of meridional ammonia structure at depths well below condensation favours an active dynamic atmosphere rather than a static layered one.

Outlook

Juno's extended mission continues through 2025 with progressively-different orbital geometry that allows MWR coverage of regions undersampled in the prime mission. The follow-on Europa Clipper (NASA, 2024 launch) is targeted at a Jovian moon rather than the planet itself but adds context for Jupiter system-wide observation. The proposed Jupiter Probe descent mission, similar in concept to the 1995 Galileo Probe but with modern instrumentation, would provide ground-truth in-situ atmospheric chemistry; no funded mission of this design is currently in development.
